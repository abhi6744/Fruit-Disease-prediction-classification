India is among the world's largest producers of fruits, with mango, apple, and citrus (orange) being three of its most economically important crops. The combined annual production of these three fruits exceeds 40 million metric tonnes, supporting millions of farming households across the country. Despite this scale, crop losses due to foliar diseases remain a persistent problem, with conservative estimates placing disease-related losses between 20–40% of total yield in affected regions.
The conventional approach to disease diagnosis involves physical inspection by trained agronomists or plant pathologists. While effective, this method has significant limitations: expert availability is geographically uneven, diagnostic turnaround times can delay treatment, and the cost of consultation is prohibitive for small farmers. These factors combine to make timely disease management difficult, especially in rural and remote farming communities.
The rapid proliferation of smartphones in rural India — with over 750 million active users as of 2024 — presents an unprecedented opportunity to democratize agricultural diagnostics. By combining the image recognition capabilities of deep learning with the ubiquity of Android smartphones, it becomes possible to deliver instant, offline disease detection directly into the hands of farmers.
This project addresses this need by developing a complete end-to-end system: from training CNN models on labeled leaf disease datasets, to deploying those models inside a user-friendly Android application. The system targets three major crops (mango, apple, orange) and covers nine disease classes, providing not just a diagnosis but also practical remediation information to guide farmers in their response.
